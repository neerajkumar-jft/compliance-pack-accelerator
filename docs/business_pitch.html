Why this matters now

India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act became law in August 2023. The executive Rules were notified in January 2025, and the Data Protection Board of India began accepting complaints and issuing penalty orders from that point onward.[1] For any Indian enterprise that holds customer, employee, or patient personal data, the question is no longer whether to comply — it's whether the compliance posture holds up to a Data Protection Board inquiry.

What the Act actually penalises (Schedule, §33)

ViolationMax penalty
Failure to take reasonable security safeguards to prevent a personal-data breach₹250 crore
Failure to notify the Board or affected principals of a breach₹200 crore
Failure to fulfil obligations regarding children's data₹200 crore
Failure to fulfil obligations of a Significant Data Fiduciary₹150 crore
Catch-all — breach of any other provision of the Act or Rules₹50 crore

The Board can impose these on a per-incident basis. One material breach → one penalty up to ₹250 crore. Repeat incidents or distinct violation types → separate penalties that stack.

For reference, India hasn't yet produced a landmark DPDP fine, but comparable regimes have. GDPR — which DPDP broadly follows — has produced €1.2 billion (Meta, 2023)[2], €746 million (Amazon, 2021)[3], and €345 million (TikTok, 2023, children's data)[4]. Indian enterprises are not immune: pre-DPDP, Mobikwik allegedly exposed 99 million users' records (2021)[5], Domino's India had 180 million order records leak (2021)[6], Air India lost data on 4.5 million passengers (2021)[7]. Under DPDP, any of those would have triggered a ₹250-crore-class inquiry.

Security — what this platform protects

The platform implements four independent enforcement layers against DPDP exposure. Each layer is Databricks-native — no bolt-on app, no custom ACL engine, no bypassable middleware. Each is verifiable in front of a regulator.

Layer 2 — column-level masking (DPDP §5(2) data minimization)

Sensitive PII columns (email, phone, Aadhaar, PAN, passport, medical diagnosis) are masked at the Unity Catalog layer. A marketing analyst querying the customer table sees o****@****.net, not the real email. The mask applies to every query path — SQL editor, dashboard tile, Genie question, REST API, notebook — not just the UI.

Layer 3 — persona-scoped access (CCO / GC / CMO / CFO)

Four role-specific dashboards and four role-scoped Genie agents. Each persona sees only the data relevant to their role. Layer 4 — DPDP §18 audit trail

Every query — by any user, from any UI path — is captured in Databricks' system audit log with user identity, timestamp, and the exact SQL. When a regulator asks "who accessed patient record XYZ on March 15?", the DPO runs one query and gets a live answer in seconds. Compare to the industry norm of extracting and reconstructing from application logs, which takes weeks and rarely reconstructs completely.

Consent is a platform capability, not just a marketing feature

The same consent layer that answers "can I email this customer?" answers every downstream personal-data question across the enterprise. The has_active_consent() function is purpose-scoped and callable from anywhere — ML training pipelines, recommendation engines, product personalization layers, third-party enrichment flows. Each downstream system stops reimplementing its own consent logic and delegates to a single source of truth.

One function, six purposes, every downstream system

[Recommendation engine] Can we use customer_04217's clickstream for model training?
has_active_consent('customer_04217', 'analytics') → true. OK to include.
[Marketing] Can we email customer_04217 about the new loyalty offer?
has_active_consent('customer_04217', 'marketing_email') → false. Withdrawn 2026-04-12. Do not email.
[Partner enrichment] Can we share this customer's profile with data-partner X?
has_active_consent('customer_04217', 'third_party_sharing') → false. Declined at capture. Do not share.

Every consented action in the enterprise passes through the same gate. Withdrawals propagate in real time via Delta CDF — downstream systems react the moment a principal withdraws, no polling, no stale state.

Delta Sharing — audit evidence without granting workspace access

When an external auditor, regulator, or DPBI inspector asks for evidence, the common failure mode is: give them a workspace account, watch them struggle with SQL, worry about what they'll see. This platform uses Databricks Delta Sharing to share read-only, time-boxed, auditor-scoped views (PII register, compliance gaps, audit log slice) — the auditor queries from their own workspace or tool of choice, never touches yours, and access is revocable in one command.

Auditor workflow, start to finish

  1. DPO creates a Delta Share named dpdp_audit_2026_q2 containing the PII register + compliance gap list + audit-log slice for the relevant period.
  2. Auditor's email is added as a recipient; they receive an activation link.
  3. Auditor queries the shared views from their own Databricks workspace (or any Delta Sharing client — DuckDB, Tableau, Power BI).
  4. After the audit concludes, the DPO revokes the recipient — auditor's access is immediately invalidated; no residual workspace accounts to clean up.

Zero additional infrastructure. Zero data copies. Zero risk of orphan auditor accounts. Standard enterprise-audit workflow replaced with an idiomatic Databricks pattern.

Operational efficiency — what this platform saves

DPDP compliance isn't a one-time project; it's an ongoing cost centre. Typical large Indian enterprises spend ₹2–10 crore annually on compliance tooling, privacy consulting, and dedicated DPO staff[8]. Much of that spend is repetitive, knowledge-worker time that this platform automates or eliminates.

Saving 1 — DPIA automation

A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) is a structured review of how personal data is processed for a given purpose. Under DPDP's Significant Data Fiduciary obligations and GDPR Article 35, DPIAs are required for high-risk processing activities. Large enterprises typically conduct 15–30 DPIAs per year.

Saving 2 — self-service compliance access

Every compliance question a business user has today creates a ticket for the DPO or a data analyst. Aggregate across a hundred-person compliance org, and it's thousands of analyst-hours per quarter spent answering questions that are lookups, not analysis.

Four persona-specific dashboards + Genie spaces. The CMO asks Genie directly; the CCO pulls the gap list themselves; the CFO sees penalty exposure without emailing anyone.

Can I email customer_04217 for next week's campaign?
No — customer_04217 withdrew marketing-email consent on 2026-04-12 via mobile app. Analytics consent is still active, but marketing communications are not authorised.

Answered in 5 seconds by a CMO from their Genie space — without a ticket, without waiting, without touching raw PII.

Saving 3 — faster regulatory response

DPDP's breach-notification clock is 72 hours from discovery under the draft Rules[10]. DSR (Data Subject Request) fulfillment SLA is commonly interpreted as 30 days. These are hard deadlines; missing them triggers §33 penalties independently of the underlying incident.
